The most important part of subtraction is to first determine which is the larger number magnitude wise

If the top number is greater magnitude wise the answer will be negative, and mechanical borrowing without considering that will give you a misleading answer, which would in turn require another layer of arithmetic afterwards

 67 
-97 

mechanical borrowing gives us 88 as the answer to this however it should be (-12)
but we got this 88 because we borrowed an extra 1 from the 100's place in 67, which did not exist so basically we augmented our top numbers magnitude by 100 
so in the end we had to subtract the answer (88) obtained from 100, to get our actual answer, 12 and then add the negative sign to it

This added arithmetic layer just increases the complexity of arithmetic for us 

AN ALTERNATIVE 

- check for the greater magnitude number and ensure that it is at the top
- then perform subtraction 
- if a swap took place in step 1 add a -ve sign to your answer 

The digits alone do not tell the whole story, we must always look at the original problem to understand wether our results should be positive or negative 

In programming signed and unsigned representations help us to enforce this distinction automatically using sign flags and bit encoding 

This is precisely why sign bits and two's plummet/complement exist 

This logic is a bit different but at the heart of how subtraction circuits work. 

Understanding borrowing will also enable us to understand how subtraction creates flags in CPU just like addition does
