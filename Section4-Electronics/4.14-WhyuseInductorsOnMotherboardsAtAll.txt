An inductor is a fundamental passive electronic component that stores energy in a magnetic field. This magnetic field forms whenever a current flows through the coil of wire in an inductor.

Inductors reduces sudden changes in electric current.

HOW DOES AN INDUCTOR WORK?
Initially an inductor acts like an open wire, barely allowing any current to flow, slowly as the magnetic field is fully established, it acts like a simple piece of wire offering low resistance, allowing almost all of the current to flow, (short wire or a DC in a ready state)

Now this behavior is essential in controlling and smoothing electrical signals, especially in fast switching circuits.
Real World Usage of Inductors are on your computers motherboard, a donut shaped component wrapped in a copper wire. 

Inductors near a CPU are mainly used in the VRM's (Voltage Regulation Circuit) to smooth current which indirectly stabilizes the voltage that is supplied to the CPU.
WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENS UNDERNEATH THE HOOD 
Inside the CPU's power delivery system the VRM (Voltage Regulator Module) 
1- Power from the PSU (12V) is converted into a very low voltage (1V) needed by the CPU.
2- The VRM uses MOSFET's, capacitors, and inductors.
3- The Inductor (also called a choke):
	- Stores energy in a magnetic field 
	- Smooths the current coming from the switching circuit.
	- Reduces current ripple and sudden spikes.


Without them tiny fluctuations in power can cause data corruption, crashes or even hardware damage.

Inductors are critical because they stabilize the current preventing sudden spikes that could disrupt sensitive microprocessors.
